Licensed Psychoanalyst

People come to therapy for many different reasons. At times, anxiety or depression can begin to feel overwhelming or difficult to manage. Other times, you may find that your life isn't unfolding in the way you had hoped, or someone close to you encourages you to seek support. Whatever brings you here, I see therapy as a meaningful investment in yourself, an opportunity to better understand who you are and how you relate to others.

Therapy is a collaborative process that can open new possibilities, deepen self-awareness, and shift the way you experience your relationships. My goal is to help you move toward a more authentic way of living -- one that feels grounded, meaningful, and fulfilling.

My approach is psychodynamic, relational, and person-centered. I work to create a supportive and secure space where a genuine therapeutic relationship can develop, as this connection is central to meaningful change. You bring a unique set of identities, experiences, and circumstances, and my role is to understand your perspective and the challenges you face. I see you as the expert on your own life, while I serve as a guide -- helping to recognize patterns, deepen insight, and bring awareness to the narratives that may no longer serve you. Together, we work toward building resilience, cultivating meaning, and opening the door to new possibilities.

I work with adults and couples, with areas of focus that include depression, anxiety, relationship concerns, fertility challenges, and the transition to parenthood.

Philosophy

My approach

My approach to therapy is primarily psychoanalytic. People often wonder how this differs from more cognitive or behavioral approaches, and the distinction is meaningful. Cognitive behavioral therapy tends to focus on identifying patterns in thoughts and behaviors that are more immediately accessible and conscious. From there, the work often involves developing practical strategies to address symptoms in a direct and structured way. This approach can be very effective, particularly when someone is looking for tools to manage specific difficulties.

… …

A psychoanalytic approach looks at symptoms from a different perspective. Rather than seeing them as problems to eliminate, it understands them as expressions of underlying emotional conflicts, often outside of awareness. Feelings like anxiety, for example, don't arise randomly; they can serve a psychological function, even if they are distressing. In this work, we try to understand not only what you are experiencing, but why. As these deeper layers come into focus, symptoms often begin to shift in a more lasting way.

… …

This process involves developing a greater awareness of your inner world, your patterns, your emotional responses, and the meanings that have taken shape over time. When these patterns are better understood, there is often more freedom to respond differently, rather than feeling caught in repetition.

… …

While my training is grounded in psychoanalysis, I don't see therapy as one-size-fits-all. Each person brings different needs, goals, and ways of engaging in the process. At times, a more structured or practical approach can be helpful, and I integrate those elements when appropriate. At the same time, I hold that deeper, insight oriented work is an important foundation for meaningful and lasting change.

Training

My Background

My clinical work is grounded in my training at the National Institute for the Psychotherapies, where I trained as a relationally oriented psychotherapist and psychoanalyst. This five-year intensive program included advanced coursework, supervised clinical work, and personal analysis, and continues to shape how I understand and engage in the therapeutic process.

… …

I studied psychology as an undergraduate, where I first developed an interest in understanding emotional life and human behavior. Before entering the field of psychotherapy, I spent a decade working in finance and completed an MBA, an experience that continues to inform my understanding of the pressures, transitions, and complexities that arise in both professional and personal life.
