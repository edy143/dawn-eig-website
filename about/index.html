My Approach

My approach to therapy is primarily psychoanalytic. People often wonder how this differs from more cognitive or behavioral approaches, and the distinction is meaningful. Cognitive behavioral therapy tends to focus on identifying patterns in thoughts and behaviors that are more immediately accessible and conscious. From there, the work often involves developing practical strategies to address symptoms in a direct and structured way.

A psychoanalytic approach looks at symptoms from a different perspective. Rather than seeing them as problems to eliminate, it understands them as expressions of underlying emotional conflicts, often outside of awareness. Feelings like anxiety, for example, don't arise randomly; they can serve a psychological function, even if they are distressing.

While my training is grounded in psychoanalysis, I don't see therapy as one-size-fits-all. Each person brings different needs, goals, and ways of engaging in the process.

My Background

I received my psychoanalytic training at the National Institute for the Psychotherapies, where I trained as a relationally oriented psychotherapist and psychoanalyst. This intensive program included advanced coursework, clinical training, supervision, and personal analysis, and continues to inform my approach to therapeutic work.

I earned my Bachelor's degree in Psychology from the University of Colorado Boulder, where I first developed an interest in understanding human behavior and emotional life. I later completed my MBA and spent a decade working in finance before transitioning into the field of psychotherapy.

This combination of experiences continues to shape my perspective, particularly in understanding the complexities of both personal and professional life, as well as the many transitions people navigate over time.
