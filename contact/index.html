Individual Therapy

Psychotherapy is a shared process — an encounter between two minds working together to make sense of what feels difficult or overwhelming. At certain moments in life, it becomes hard to navigate things on your own. In those moments, having another person alongside you can open up new ways of understanding. Together, we begin to ask: what are the stories you carry about yourself, and how have they taken shape over time? As we explore these narratives, it often becomes clear that some no longer fit and may be ready to shift.

When painful experiences remain outside of awareness, they tend to repeat themselves in subtle and not-so-subtle ways. These patterns can begin to feel stuck, as if you're circling the same struggles without resolution. Therapy offers a space to pause these cycles, to look at them more closely, and to begin imagining something different — so that change feels possible and life becomes more manageable and meaningful.

People often wonder whether talking alone can truly make a difference. In many cases, it can. Research and clinical experience alike show that meaningful change can emerge through conversation, especially when approached with openness and curiosity. Over time, the therapeutic relationship itself becomes a place where new ways of thinking, feeling, and relating can take shape.

You might think of therapy as creating room to think and feel more freely — especially when anxiety, depression, or overwhelm make it hard to find clarity. Within this space, new perspectives can emerge, along with a deeper understanding of yourself and your experiences. As this unfolds, many people begin to feel a sense of relief and greater emotional freedom.

This process is guided by someone trained to understand emotional pain — how it develops, how it is expressed, and how it can be worked through with care and attention. Therapy makes it possible to access parts of yourself that may have been out of reach, shaped by past experiences or held outside of awareness. Bringing these elements into view is often an important step toward lasting change.

Individual Psychoanalysis

Psychoanalysis began with Freud at the end of the 19th century, but it has evolved significantly over time. Today, it is a living, dynamic practice shaped by more than a century of clinical experience and new ways of understanding the mind.

Contemporary psychoanalysis centers on the relationship between you and the analyst. It uses that relationship as a way to explore your inner world — your thoughts, feelings, memories, and patterns — in a sustained and meaningful way. The work is immersive and deeply collaborative, grounded in a shared commitment to understanding your experience more fully. Within this process, space is created for insight, connection, and change, allowing for a deeper engagement with areas of conflict, pain, and possibility.

One of the primary distinctions between psychotherapy and psychoanalysis is the frequency of sessions. While this may seem like a practical detail, it has important implications for the depth of the work. Meeting more frequently allows for a greater sense of continuity and immersion, making it easier to notice patterns as they unfold both within the sessions and in your daily life. This sustained engagement can deepen the process and open up more opportunities for meaningful change.

At the same time, once-weekly psychotherapy can be highly effective and transformative. The choice of frequency depends on what feels manageable and appropriate for you. When it is possible to meet more often, however, the added consistency and intensity can strengthen the therapeutic relationship and support a more in-depth exploration.

Couples Therapy

Couples therapy is a space where the relationship itself becomes the focus of our work. Rather than concentrating on one person, we pay attention to the dynamic that unfolds between you, how you communicate, where you feel stuck, and how patterns develop over time. As a team, we work toward understanding these patterns with greater clarity and care.

Part of this process involves learning how each of you experiences and expresses emotion. Often, partners can feel misunderstood even when there is genuine care and effort. Together, we begin to "translate" each other's emotional languages, recognizing that empathy in a relationship goes beyond imagining how you might feel in the same situation. It involves drawing on a deeper understanding of your partner, their history, sensitivities, and ways of making meaning, to more fully grasp their experience.

Many couples find themselves caught in repeating cycles, miscommunications, frustrations, or disconnections that seem to resolve temporarily but then return. In therapy, we slow these moments down to better understand what is happening beneath the surface. This creates an opportunity to respond differently, with more awareness and intention, rather than reacting patterns.

Couples therapy also creates space to rebuild trust, strengthen connection, and re-engage with one another in more meaningful ways. This may involve working through conflict, navigating life transitions, or addressing longstanding tensions. Over time, the goal is not only to reduce distress, but to help you feel more seen, understood, and supported within the relationship.

My approach to therapy is primarily psychoanalytic. People often wonder how this differs from more cognitive or behavioral approaches, and the distinction is meaningful. Cognitive behavioral therapy tends to focus on identifying patterns in thoughts and behaviors that are more immediately accessible and conscious. From there, the work often involves developing practical strategies to address symptoms in a direct and structured way. This approach can be very effective, particularly when someone is looking for tools to manage specific difficulties.

A psychoanalytic approach looks at symptoms from a different perspective. Rather than seeing them as problems to eliminate, it understands them as expressions of underlying emotional conflicts, often outside of awareness. Feelings like anxiety, for example, don't arise randomly; they can serve a psychological function, even if they are distressing. In this work, we try to understand not only what you are experiencing, but why. As these deeper layers come into focus, symptoms often begin to shift in a more lasting way.

This process involves developing a greater awareness of your inner world, your patterns, your emotional responses, and the meanings that have taken shape over time. When these patterns are better understood, there is often more freedom to respond differently, rather than feeling caught in repetition.

While my training is grounded in psychoanalysis, I don't see therapy as one-size-fits-all. Each person brings different needs, goals, and ways of engaging in the process. At times, a more structured or practical approach can be helpful, and I integrate those elements when appropriate. At the same time, I hold that deeper, insight oriented work is an important foundation for meaningful and lasting change.

I received my psychoanalytic training at the National Institute for the Psychotherapies, where I trained as a relationally oriented psychotherapist and psychoanalyst. This intensive program included advanced coursework, clinical training, supervision, and personal analysis, and continues to inform my approach to therapeutic work.

I earned my Bachelor's degree in Psychology from the University of Colorado Boulder, where I first developed an interest in understanding human behavior and emotional life. I later completed my MBA and spent a decade working in finance before transitioning into the field of psychotherapy.

Alongside this path, I engaged in graduate-level study in clinical psychology over a number of years, deepening my academic and clinical understanding prior to beginning my formal psychoanalytic training.

This combination of experiences continues to shape my perspective, particularly in understanding the complexities of both personal and professional life, as well as the many transitions people navigate over time.
